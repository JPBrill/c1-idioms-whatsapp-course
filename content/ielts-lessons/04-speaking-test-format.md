The **IELTS Speaking Test** is often the most nerve-wracking part of the exam, but it's also the shortest. Whether you are taking **IELTS Academic**, **General Training**, or **UKVI**, the good news is: **the Speaking test is exactly the same for everyone.**

## Quick Overview: What to Expect

- **Duration:** 11–14 minutes.
- **Format:** One-on-one interview with a certified examiner.
- **Mode:** Face-to-face (in a quiet room) OR video call (at an official centre).
- **Recording:** Your test is recorded for marking purposes.
- **Same for All:** Academic, General, and UKVI candidates take the **exact same speaking test**.

---

## The 3 Parts of the Speaking Test

### Part 1: Introduction & Interview (4–5 Minutes)

**"The Warm-Up"**

This section is designed to help you relax. It is conversational and predictable.

- The examiner asks you to identify yourself (passport/ID check).
- Questions are simple and about **yourself** and **familiar topics**.
- **Common topics:** Home & hometown, work or study, hobbies & interests, family & friends, food, music, weather.

> **Tip:** Keep answers natural. Don't memorise speeches, but do prepare vocabulary for these common topics.

---

### Part 2: The Long Turn / Cue Card (3–4 Minutes)

**"The Monologue"**

This is often the hardest part because you must speak alone without interruption.

- The examiner gives you a **Cue Card** with a topic and 3–4 bullet points to cover.
- **Preparation:** 1 minute of silent preparation time with a pencil and paper.
- **Speaking:** You must speak for **up to 2 minutes**. The examiner stops you when time is up.
- **Follow-up:** The examiner may ask 1–2 short questions at the end.
- **Common topics:** Describe a person you admire, a place you visited, a gift you received, or a skill you learned.

> **Pro Tip:** Use the full 1 minute to write down **keywords**, not full sentences. This keeps you fluent for the full 2 minutes.

---

### Part 3: Two-Way Discussion (4–5 Minutes)

**"The Deep Dive"**

This is the most abstract and challenging part.

- Broader, more complex questions **linked to the topic in Part 2**.
- You shift from talking about *yourself* (Part 1) to talking about *society, the world, or general ideas*.
- **Example link:**
  - *Part 2:* Describe a teacher you liked.
  - *Part 3:* "How has education changed in the last 50 years?" / "Do you think technology will replace teachers?"
- **Key skills:** Expressing opinions, analysing issues, speculating about the future, evaluating arguments.

## Academic vs. General vs. UKVI: Any Differences?

| Feature | Academic | General Training | UKVI |
|---|---|---|---|
| Questions | Same | Same | Same |
| Difficulty | Same | Same | Same |
| Scoring | Same | Same | Same |
| Examiners | Same | Same | Same |
| Security | Standard | Standard | **Video recorded** |

If you take **IELTS for UKVI**, the content is identical. The only difference is that the session is **video recorded** for extra security verification required by the UK Home Office.

---

## How Are You Scored? (The 4 Criteria)

You are not graded on your intelligence or opinions. You are graded on your **language**. Each criterion is worth **25%**:

1. **Fluency and Coherence (FC):** Can you speak at length without too many pauses? Do you connect ideas logically?
2. **Lexical Resource (LR):** Do you use a wide range of vocabulary, idioms, and collocations accurately?
3. **Grammatical Range and Accuracy (GRA):** Do you use a mix of simple and complex sentence structures with correct grammar?
4. **Pronunciation (P):** Is your speech easy to understand? Do you use intonation and stress naturally?

---

> The IELTS Speaking test is an interaction, not an interrogation. Smile, be confident, and treat it like a conversation with a stranger in a coffee shop — albeit a formal one!
